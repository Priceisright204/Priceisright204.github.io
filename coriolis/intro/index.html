Life on a Spinning Space Station

No one has ever built a spinning space station before. By spinning a space station like a centrifuge, astronauts could be held to the floor like gravity. What would it be like in such a place?

Objects move differently in a spinning environment due to a phenomena called the Coriolis effect. If you were to toss something in the air, it would not land where you expect it to. This is because the object you throw takes a shortcut through the air, while you continue to travel the long way around the edge. In effect, movement will look distorted.

My computer model shows motion on a spinning space station. The next page (“Fun things to do with the model”) gives you some ideas for interesting throws to use with the model.

Whether or not we should build a spinning space station is up to history to decide.

Should We Build A Spinning Space Station?

There are arguments both for and against building a spinning space station.

Creating a spinning environment would be expensive, and my model shows that a spinning station might not match up to our expectations. It might prove difficult to get used to perpetual spinning; it is unclear how well astronauts could adapt to a spinning world. The body would need to adapt to every direction of motion differently because each direction would produce a different effect.

On the other hand, producing gravity may prove essential to counteracting the health effects of microgravity on deep-space missions. Life without gravity produces negative health effects such as vision changes, premature aging, weakening bones and muscles, and more. One of the main objectives of astronaut Scot Kelly’s recent year-long trip to space was to learn more about these health effects.

I believe that we should build a spinning space station someday for the purpose of learning more about this type of environment. We cannot know if this environment will be helpful to astronauts if we never try it.
