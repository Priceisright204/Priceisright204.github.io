How does the Coriolis effect work?

The Coriolis effect causes objects in a rotating environment to move where you do not expect them to. To understand why you must take a look at the movement from a perspective that is not rotating.

If you were to jump in artificial gravity, the centripetal force at your feet would cease the instant you leave the floor. You would observe Coriolis “forces” as you “accelerate” back to the floor; however, an onlooker who is not spinning would see you move in a straight line.

Now we will return to the coin toss. The graphic above shows what a vertical coin toss looks like to an observer outside the station. The coin cuts through the middle by going in a straight line; the thrower continues moving with the floor, taking the longer path.

The Coriolis force is different based on the direction an object is moving. This is what you would see:

  • Objects moving with the rotation would feel an extra downward “force.” They are effectively rotating more quickly.

  • Objects moving opposite the rotation would become “lighter” or would move “up.” If you matched the station’s speed, you would become weightless.

  • Objects moving “upward” would be pushed into the rotation.

  • Objects moving “downward” would be pushed opposite the rotation.

The larger the station, the smaller these Coriolis effects become; however, to make the effects unnoticeable, you would need an impractically massive space station! I have modeled our coin toss in a logarithmic graph. Earth-“normalness” improves incredibly slowly, on a logarithmic scale.

This assumes the throw starts from four feet and goes to a total height of seven feet.
