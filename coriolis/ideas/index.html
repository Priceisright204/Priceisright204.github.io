Here are some things you can do with the model.

  • Try dropping the coin instead of throwing it (throw it at 0 ft/s). It curves in the opposite direction.

  • Next, try dropping the coin from almost the center of the station. With the diameter at 50 feet, place the starting height at 24.5 feet. Imagine you are standing on a tall pole and drop the coin while rotating near the center. It appears to spiral outward slowly due to the low gravity near the center. The coin actually travels in a straight path, but the floor–where we are observing from–rotates relative to the coin.

A gargantuan virtual firing range

Tomahawk cruise missile

Let’s create a massive rotating firing range with a diameter of 10,000 km. As you increase the size of a station, in theory, the error due to the Coriolis effect decreases. This section will show you that even the most massive space stations have visible error due to the Coriolis effect.

We will try cruise missiles and bullets at both long-range and short-range. A bullet’s speed is close to the speed of the floor, which means it will follow a distorted path.

Try it!

Diameter=10000000 m

Start height=1.2 m

Angle=90°

Speed=250 m/s

Gravity=100%

  • Try firing a missile (250 m/s) at 90 degrees from vertical. This is a long-range shot; you are firing straight into the distance. The projectile would land about 5 m (15 feet) off-target! For this test, I assume the missiles are not guided.

  • Change the launch angle by just a degree, and the result changes dramatically! This button repeats the long-range shot at 89 degrees. Now you see 17 meters (56 feet) of error.

  • Now try a short-range shot. Adjust the model to 100 degrees from vertical (10 degrees below horizontal) to aim the projectile at the ground. It would land about a millimeter from its intended target, 6.8 meters away.

In summary, long-range firing on a massive station would be very difficult. The error would be different depending on the direction you are facing, the velocity of the projectile, and the size of the station. Nonetheless, a coin toss would appear as it would on Earth, and short-range shots would be very precise. A guided missile would always find its intended target, even with the Coriolis effect.
