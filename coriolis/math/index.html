The math behind this model: “Difference from throw on Earth”

As I began putting this computer model together, I wanted a simple go-to measurement for the difference between a rotating throw and a throw on Earth. This provided me a fascinating physics problem. My goal was to calculate, if a person were to toss a coin in a rotating environment, how far away would it land from their feet?

Set-up the problem

To fully understand what happens during our coin toss, we must use a perspective that is not rotating. When beginning to solve a physics problem, it is important to establish a coordinate system. This helps to make sense of what is going on.

The station is a virtual coordinate system with (0,0) at its exact center. I imagined the station rotating clockwise.

Here are some conditions for this coin toss:

  • At the moment of the throw, the person is standing at the bottom of the circle, at 270 degrees or 3π/2 radians.
  • The person is holding the coin at 4 feet from the floor. This is the starting height.
  • The person throws the coin straight upward at 14 ft/s, which would have sent the coin about 3 feet up in the air on Earth.
  • The rotating space station has a diameter of 50 feet. It is rotating at the speed necessary to produce 1g of acceleration, or 100% Earth gravity at the floor.
  • The coin experiences less than this amount of centripetal acceleration or “gravity” because it is not at the floor.

Before the throw, the coin has a leftwards velocity due to the clockwise rotation; the throw adds an upwards velocity of 14 ft/s (the throw speed). These two velocity components define the direction that the coin travels through the air, which we will need to calculate so we can determine where it lands.

The picture below is a diagram of these velocity components. $V$ represents the magnitude and direction of the coin’s velocity, and $V_x$ and $V_y$ are its components. $V_x$ equals the speed the coin was rotating at before the throw, and $V_y$ is 14 ft/s. $F_c$ is the centripetal force which disappears at the moment the coin is released.

Now that I have set up the problem, I will explain the next steps. Ideally, I would like a single equation that can solve the problem for me. Unfortunately, such an equation would be messy and impossible to simplify, so we will solve this in steps. We want to find how far away a coin would land from the thrower’s feet. To find this, we need to find the coordinates of where both the thrower and the coin would end up.

First, we need the magnitude and direction of the coin’s velocity after it launches. Using this, we can solve for its landing coordinate and the time it elapsed in the air. We can then use the time elapsed in the air to find where the thrower would end up in an equivalent time period. We can use this to find the separation between the coin and the thrower’s feet.

Here are the steps to solving our math problem:

  1. Calculate the rotation speed at the floor in radians per second
  2. Use the rotation speed to calculate the horizontal speed of the coin in feet per second
  3. Calculate the magnitude and direction of the coin’s overall velocity through the air
  4. Find the coin’s landing coordinates
  5. Calculate the time elapsed as the coin traveled through the air
  6. Calculate the arc swept by the thrower during this same time
  7. Calculate the thrower’s final coordinates
  8. Calculate the distance between the thrower’s feet and the coin when the coin lands


Starting Velocity

We need the vertical and horizontal velocity components of the coin. The vertical velocity has been given to us as 14 ft/s, the throw speed of the coin. The horizontal velocity is currently unknown.

Before we solve for the coin’s initial horizontal velocity, please note that the coin does not feel the same force of gravity as you do on the floor of the station. It has a smaller radius to the center of the station because you are holding it in your hand; the coin starts at a height of 4 ft from the floor. Thus, the coin’s horizontal velocity would not be quite the same as if it were touching the floor.

The floor’s “gravity” is a constant acceleration toward the center. This is the equation for centripetal acceleraton:

Variables:

  • a is the acceleration felt by the object in ft/s/s
  • $v$ is the horizontal (tangential) velocity of an object on the station in ft/s
  • r is the radius, or distance from the object to the center of rotation in feet

Unfortunately, we do not know either the acceleration felt by the coin or its current horizontal velocity. This is why we will need the rotational speed at the floor. The rotational speed in radians per second will be equivalent at the floor and at the coin. We do know the acceleration felt at the floor.

Rotational, or angular, velocity is related to linear velocity. This relationship is defined by $v=r*\omega$ where $\omega$ is the rotational rate of the station in radians/second.

We can use this with our centripetal acceleration equation to get rotational speed.

In our formula:

  • $a=-32.174$ ft/s/s, the acceleration at the floor
  • $r$ is the radius of the station, or 25 feet.
  • $\omega$ is the rotational speed in radians per second

We can then use $\omega$ to calculate the horizontal velocity of the coin. The radius at the coin will be 25 ft minus 4 ft, or 21 feet. Therefore, $v_x=21*\omega$. This value will be negative because the coin is traveling left and is rotating clockwise.

Our variables:

  • a = the acceleration of the station, -32.174 ft/s/s

  • $h_{c}$ = the starting height of the coin


The Coin’s Landing Coordinates

We can use the velocity components of the coin to find its landing coordinates. The coin is traveling up and to the left in a straight line; the slope of the path it follows is the fraction of the two velocity components, $\frac{v_y}{v_x}$.

The equation for a line: $y = mx + b$

Our variables:

  • $m=\frac{v_y}{v_x}$, it will be negative.
  • $b=$ the y-intercept $=-(r_{s}-h_{c})$

Time Elapsed in the Air

Now that we have the landing coordinates, we can solve for the exact time elapsed using kinematics equations. First, we need the distance traveled. We will calculate this using the Pythagorean Theorem. The final coordinates of the coin are (x_f, y_f), and the initial coordinates are (0,$-r_{c}$).

Pythagorean theorem: $d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$.

Using our variables: $d=\sqrt{x_f^2+(y_f+r_{c})^2}$

We also need to solve for the velocity. We have the x and y components. The velocity, with the Pythagorean theorem, is $v=\sqrt {v_x^2 + v_y^2}$.

The Arc Swept By the Person

We can use the time variable to calculate the angle a person will cover while the coin is in the air. This will give us the final coordinates of the person.

We have already calculated the station’s rotation rate in the beginning of our problem. This is $\omega$, in radians/second.

The person starts at an angle of $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ (270 degrees). The person is sweeping a negative arc, because they are traveling clockwise (counterclockwise is positive for angles).

If $\theta_t$ is the total arc swept by the person, then you would expect $\theta_t=\theta_f-\theta_0$. However, you must keep in mind the negative direction of the arc being traveled. Max Height Achieved

My table also displays the max height achieved by the coin. Unfortunately, there is no formula to calculate this. I generated this number by creating a maximization script; the script sweeps up and down the path of the coin to find the point that is at the farthest distance from the circle.
