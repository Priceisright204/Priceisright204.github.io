What is this model?

This model demonstrates how much artificial gravity would alter the environment around you on a space station. My model shows that, thanks to the Coriolis effect, artificial gravity would be very strange; it would not look like Earth’s gravity.

You can change the throw to whatever you want by pushing the “Edit Variables” button on the model page. The next page will give you some ideas (“Fun things to do with the computer model”).

What is artificial gravity?

We can simulate gravity by spinning; this is “artificial” gravity. If you swing a bucket of water fast enough around your head, the water will stay inside. This creates what is called the centripetal force.

Unfortunately, we do not currently have a space station that generates gravity, but the physics behind artificial gravity is real and could be put into practice in space.

What is the Coriolis effect?

The Coriolis effect is an effect of artificial gravity that causes objects to move where you do not expect them to. Objects that are rotating do not behave the same as objects that are not. This effect can also be seen in Earth’s weather patterns.

Because of this effect, life in simulated gravity may prove difficult to get used to. The Coriolis effect would affect everything you do. Increasing the size of a space station reduces the Coriolis effect, but eliminating it entirely would require an impossibly large space station.

Model Assumptions

This model assumes:

  • there is no air resistance
  • the numbers in the input menu represent the throw you would have made on Earth
  • before the throw, the coin was rotating with the thrower’s hand

The model shows the thrower’s perspective. The space station’s floor looks stationary to him. He sees outer space rotating counterclockwise.
